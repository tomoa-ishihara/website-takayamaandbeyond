What to expect
01
The entrance
You walk through the garden and enter through a traditional nijiriguchi — a small, low entrance that requires you to bow. The gesture is intentional. You leave the outside world behind.
02
Receiving tea
The master prepares a bowl of tea. There is no explanation at first — you receive the tea while observing his movements. The silence is part of it.
03
Preparing tea yourself
You move to a larger tatami room. He explains the fundamentals and guides you through preparing tea yourself — the folding of the cloth, the warming of the bowl, the motion. It takes longer than you expect.

The Place

A private tea house near a shrine.

The experience takes place across an entire private tea house, 10 minutes on foot from Takayama's old town. The master regularly hosts tea gatherings here. You begin by walking through the garden and entering through a traditional nijiriguchi entrance. The entire ground floor is dedicated to tea, with multiple rooms designed for different parts of the practice. Guests are given access to this normally private space — not as a display, but as a place in use.

[ Photo / Video ]
The Hosts

A tea master
within a living
lineage..

The host practices the Omotesenke 表千家 tradition, one of the main schools of Japanese tea. His family is connected to the lineage of Sen no Rikyu, whose philosophy still defines tea today. This is not a performance. It is part of his daily life — something he continues, not something he recreates.

The Centrepiece

Matcha.

Made by a master.

Prepared correctly — the right amount of water, the right temperature, the right motion — matcha has a depth and sweetness that powdered matcha from a tin cannot replicate. You'll observe the master prepare it first, then prepare it yourself. With wagashi — traditional Japanese sweets — to balance the bitterness. You'll taste the difference immediately.
